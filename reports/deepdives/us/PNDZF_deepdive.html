03The IPO Stage — And What Would Change My View
Technical analysis follows the stage framework described in Trade Like a Stock Market Wizard by Mark Minervini (McGraw-Hill, 2013), adapted for US equities. Stage 4 means the price is below key moving averages — it reflects price action, not business quality.

PNDZF is currently in an `ipo_primary_base` technical stage, with a `TT SCORE` of 0/8 and a `SIGNAL` of `ipo_stress_test_required`. This means the stock is relatively new to the public markets and is still in the process of forming its initial price base. It hasn't established a clear Stage 2 uptrend or a Stage 4 downtrend. The "stress test required" signal indicates that I need to see how the stock performs under various market conditions and how it consolidates its initial price action before a clear trend can be identified. This is a normal and necessary period for IPOs, where the market is absorbing shares and finding a fair value. My focus here is on observing the stability of this base and the stock's ability to hold key support levels.

What Would Upgrade My View

  • Clear establishment of a Stage 2 uptrend, characterized by price moving above key moving averages with increasing volume, signaling institutional accumulation.
  • Successful "stress testing" of the IPO base, where the stock demonstrates resilience during broader market pullbacks and holds critical support levels.
  • Continued robust financial performance in subsequent earnings reports, validating the strong initial metrics and demonstrating consistent execution.
  • Increased insider buying activity, signaling management's confidence in the company's future prospects.

What Would Make Me Stop Watching

  • A sustained break below the IPO base support levels, indicating significant selling pressure or a fundamental shift in market perception.
  • A noticeable and prolonged deterioration in PNDZF's exceptional profitability metrics, such as a significant decline in FCF margin or ROE.
  • Negative news regarding the company's brand reputation, product quality, or a structural loss of market share within the luxury goods sector.
  • Any signs of poor corporate governance or a sudden, unexplained increase in debt levels without a clear strategic rationale.
