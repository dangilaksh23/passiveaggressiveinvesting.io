03Why It's Stage 3 (Topping) — And What Would Change That
Technical analysis follows the stage framework described in Trade Like a Stock Market Wizard by Mark Minervini (McGraw-Hill, 2013), adapted for US equities. Stage 4 means the price is below key moving averages — it reflects price action, not business quality.

Currently, Harmony Gold Mining is in Stage 3, indicating a topping pattern with a low TT Score of 2/8. This means the stock price is likely experiencing increased volatility, potentially forming a distribution pattern or consolidating after a significant uptrend. For a gold miner, this could be influenced by broader market sentiment towards commodities, profit-taking after a strong run, or general sector rotation, rather than company-specific negative news, as none has been identified. Despite the strong fundamentals, the technical action suggests caution, as the stock is struggling to make new highs or maintain its upward momentum. I'm observing this technical weakness as a potential signal of a pause or reversal, rather than a reflection of its underlying business quality.

What Would Upgrade My View

  • I would need to see a clear resolution of the topping pattern, ideally with the stock breaking out into a new Stage 2 uptrend, supported by increasing volume and reclaiming key moving averages.
  • Continued strong operational performance, particularly sustained or improving FCF margins and ROE, reinforcing the fundamental strength regardless of short-term price action.
  • A sustained bullish macro environment for gold, driven by factors like inflation concerns, geopolitical instability, or weakening fiat currencies, providing a tailwind for the sector.
  • An improvement or stabilization in the Cash Conversion Cycle (CCC) trend, indicating better working capital management.

What Would Make Me Stop Watching

  • A confirmed breakdown into a Stage 4 downtrend, especially if accompanied by fundamental deterioration or a significant collapse in gold prices that impacts profitability.
  • Sustained and significant declines in key financial metrics, such as a sharp drop in FCF margin, operating margin, or ROE, suggesting a structural problem with the business.
  • Emergence of significant negative news, such as major operational disruptions, regulatory issues, or serious governance concerns within the company.
  • A continued, structural deterioration in the Cash Conversion Cycle that begins to materially impact the company's liquidity or free cash flow.
