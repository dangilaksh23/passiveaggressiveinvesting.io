Sector Signals from the Last Two Weeks
Agent 0 Scan · 11 Apr – 25 Apr 2026

These are observations from my news scan, not stock recommendations. They describe macro and policy developments that may influence how I think about the sectors I already track. Specific company names appear only where they are unavoidable in describing the news, not as buy or sell ideas.

Water Infrastructure — Allocation Restored, Then Disbursed

The Union Budget 2026-27 allocation to Jal Jeevan Mission was restored to ₹67,670 crore, materially higher than the FY26 revised estimate of ₹17,000 crore. On 1 April, the central government released a fresh tranche to five states totalling ₹1,561 crore under JJM, including ₹792 crore to Uttar Pradesh and ₹536 crore to Chhattisgarh. The pattern of underutilisation in earlier years remains a real risk, but the directional signal for the sector is positive. AMRUT 2.0 outlay is ₹2,77,000 crore total over the FY22–FY26 mission window, with the central share funded from the union budget. Cleared sanctions under AMRUT 2.0 already include 11,393 megaliters per day of water treatment capacity, 17.8 million new tap connections, and 6,649 megaliters per day of sewage treatment capacity.

What this means for my framework: water remains a strong Layer 1 sector. The companies I already track in this space stay in the watchlist with no change in regulatory conviction. New companies may surface in the next universe build.

Power Distribution & Cables — RDSS Allocation Up, Smart Metering Slow

The Revamped Distribution Sector Scheme allocation increased in the FY27 budget. Funding rose from ₹15,671 crore in the prior fiscal to ₹18,000 crore for 2026-27. The Ministry of Power overall received ₹29,997 crore, a 39% increase over the revised estimate of 2025-26, with about 60% of total expenditure allocated towards RDSS. However, smart meter rollout remains slow. As of March 2025, only 13.4 million meters (5% of the 250 million target) are currently communicating, and only about 4.8 million are in prepaid mode. Underground cabling under RDSS is being sanctioned for cyclone-resilient works in coastal regions of Maharashtra, Gujarat, Kerala, and Andhra Pradesh, with high-tension and low-tension underground cabling being a focus.

What this means for my framework: cables and conductors stays a strong Layer 1 sector. The execution gap in smart metering is a watch point — companies whose order books depend on meter pace specifically may need closer review.

Renewables & Decentralised Solar — Allocation Up 30%

The Ministry of New and Renewable Energy allocation rose 30% from the revised estimate of 2025-26 to ₹32,915 crore for FY27, driven by higher allocations toward PM Surya Ghar Muft Bijli Yojana. ₹220 billion of the renewable energy allocation goes specifically to PM Surya Ghar, the rooftop solar scheme. The push toward decentralised solar and the persistent stress on DISCOMs (which RDSS is meant to address) are the two large narratives in the power sector this budget cycle.

What this means for my framework: I will look more carefully at whether decentralised solar and rooftop-PV-adjacent companies should be added to the universe in the next pipeline run.

EV / Auto Components — Customs Exemption Extended

The 2026 Budget extended concessional customs duty and duty exemptions on lithium-ion cells and battery components for domestic EV manufacture up to 31 March 2028, originally set to expire on 31 March 2026. PLI for EV manufacturing also saw an increase. The signal is policy continuity, which is what an EV-exposed auto component thesis needs to remain intact.

What this means for my framework: auto metal forming stays at Moderate Layer 1. EV-exposed auto names retain their thesis. The customs extension is supportive, not transformative.

Why I Built PAI
A Personal Note

I studied economics. The thing that stuck with me from those years wasn't a particular model. It was a conviction that firms are not interchangeable. A small firm in its first decade faces a fundamentally different problem set than a mature large-cap. The factors that drive success at one stage of a company's life cycle are not the same factors that drive success at another. Theory of the firm, industrial organisation, network effects all tell us that the right lens depends on what kind of firm you are looking at.

Most retail investors in India are told to choose between two extremes. Passive index funds on one side, speculation on tips on the other. Neither approach uses what economics actually tells us about how firms create value. PAI is the framework I built for the middle path. Research-driven, framework-led, transparent.

I draw on what I learned as an economics student, and on time spent working in lending. Particularly in MSME lending, where you see up close how cash flows, customer concentration, working capital cycles, and informal networks actually decide whether a small business survives. Those years made me sceptical of valuation models that ignore the network around a firm. A balance sheet tells you what a company owns. It does not tell you who its customers are, who its investors are, or whether the founder has the relationships to keep the lights on through a downturn.

That observation, that network effects are systematically underweighted in standard valuation and technical frameworks, became the foundation of the C2 layer in PAI. The thinking on this layer was sharpened in conversations with a friend pursuing a PhD in economics whose research focus is on networks. I am grateful for those conversations. The framing of network quality as a scorable layer rather than soft commentary owes a lot to them.

Why I Disclose My Holdings
Skin in the Game

Most investment writing online does not show what the author personally owns. The author can talk about a company in glowing terms while quietly holding a position they want to sell into your buying. That is a conflict of interest, and it is invisible unless the author chooses to disclose it.

I disclose my own holdings on this site for that reason. When I write about a company I own, you can see that I own it. When my framework score for a company I own goes down, you can see that too. The disclosure is a check on me, not a recommendation to anyone else.

Every framework has limitations. Mine is not exempt. The PAI Score is a directional organisation tool. It can be wrong. Companies that fit the framework can lose value. Companies that do not fit can do well. I am keeping track of how my own framework performs over time so I can improve it, not so I can claim it works. The site is honest about the gap between framework and outcome.

Execution matters as much as analysis. Buying at a sensible level, holding through volatility without panic-selling, exiting when the thesis breaks rather than when the price wobbles, that is its own skill, separate from whether the framework call was sound. My holdings disclosure is partly a record of how I have done on execution, not just on analysis.

I am not selling anything. I do not run a paid newsletter, a Telegram channel, a course, or a tip service. There is no monetisation on this site, direct or indirect. The site exists because I needed it to exist for myself. It is public so other people can read along if they find the thinking useful. None of it is investment advice.

The Role of AI in This Work
Human-Guided, AI-Executed

The π in PAI is not decoration. This work would not be possible at this depth, at this speed, by one person, without AI. I want to be transparent about how AI is actually used here, because the difference between "AI-powered" as marketing and AI as a real working tool is large.

Specialised AI agents handle different parts of the pipeline. Each agent has one job, and the framework binds the outputs together into a coherent thesis.

  • 0
    News and idea scout. Continuously scans financial news, sector announcements, government scheme updates, and market chatter. Surfaces sectors and companies that look worth a closer look. This is the funnel that feeds everything else.
  • 1
    Sector and policy analyst. Reads government schemes, ministry circulars, and budget documents. Builds the L1 regulatory case that anchors the NAV framework.
  • 2
    Universe builder and network mapper. Pulls financials, identifies peer sets, maps customer concentration, institutional holdings, and promoter networks. This is where the C2 layer comes from.
  • 3
    Bucket scorer. Applies the framework rules to assign each company to a bucket and compute the PAI Score components.
  • 4
    Peer comparator. Benchmarks each candidate against its sector peers on every relevant metric, surfaces who is genuinely better and who is just cheaper.
  • 5
    Deep dive writer. Pulls everything together into a structured report. Same template every time, no narrative drift.
  • 6
    Technical analyst. Computes moving averages, evaluates the trend template, classifies the stage, identifies pivots. Replaces hand-drawn chart analysis with consistent, rule-based output.
  • 7
    Live verification monitor. Checks every price-sensitive number against a live source before publication. Catches stale data before it becomes a stale call.

Every output goes through me. I read the report, push back on the score where I disagree, override the agent when I think it has missed context, and decide what gets published. The AI is fast and consistent. The human supplies judgment, scepticism, and the willingness to throw an output away when it does not feel right.

This is also why the framework can be honest about its limits. When AI is doing the heavy lifting on consistency, the human can spend energy on the things AI is bad at. Asking whether the data is even the right data. Noticing when a company's narrative has changed in a way the numbers have not yet caught. Deciding when a high score should still not be acted on because something feels off.

How I Analyse Companies · Modular by Design
Many Firms, Many Frameworks
Modular by Design

If different kinds of firms need different lenses, no single framework can be universal. NAV is the first one. It is designed for small and mid-cap firms riding identifiable government policy tailwinds, where the regulatory anchor is a meaningful driver of revenue. It would be poorly suited to a large-cap turnaround, an early-stage IPO, or a momentum-driven breakout in a sector with no scheme behind it.

That is why this site is architected to be framework-modular. As I add new frameworks for special situations, momentum, large-cap re-rating, early-stage IPOs, they will appear as separate scoring systems. Each will have its own conviction bands, its own report format, and its own published track record. Reports will be tagged with which framework was used. Conflicting calls between frameworks will be visible, not hidden.

The PAI Score is something I built for my own use. The exact rubric for converting layer assessments into a single number is my personal tool and is not published in detail. What is public is the score, the band it falls in, the reasoning behind each layer for each company, and a clear statement that the score is not a recommendation.

Framework #1 — NAV
For Indian Small and Mid-Cap Compounders

The Four Questions

NAV asks four questions about a company, in order. The intuition is that all four matter, and any one being weak should make you pause.

  • C1
    Is there a regulatory tailwind? Government schemes that fund the sector directly are stronger than schemes that hand cash incentives to private players. Direct customer beats indirect benefit.
  • C2
    Is the network around the firm high quality? Three sub-questions. Who are the customers, who are the institutional investors, who are the promoters. The C2 layer is where the network-effects thinking lives.
  • C3
    Is the valuation defensible? P/E versus sector peers, plus percent upside to a rigorously derived target. Stocks that are cheap but offer no upside are not interesting. Expensive stocks with strong upside earn closer attention.
  • C4
    Is the chart ready? Stock structure, base formation, volume confirmation. A fundamentally good firm in a downtrend is not bought. Wait for the chart to confirm.

Score Bands

  • 1
    75–100 → Full Position. Highest conviction. All four framework criteria met.
  • 2
    55–74 → Standard. Strong on most criteria, one weaker leg.
  • 3
    35–54 → Pilot Only. Framework signals inconsistent. Track but don't deploy full capital.
  • 4
    0–34 → Do Not Buy. Does not match framework. Says nothing about price direction — just outside my framework.

Beyond the Score

  • +
    Underwriter-style diligence on adverse signals. Like a credit underwriter looking at a borrower, every company in the universe is checked for adverse news, governance flags, regulatory action, auditor changes, related-party transactions, and unusual disclosures. All of this uses publicly available data. Adverse signals do not always change the score, but they always change how closely the position is monitored.

The technical layer (C4) draws on the stage analysis and trend template approach described in Trade Like a Stock Market Wizard by Mark Minervini (McGraw-Hill, 2013), adapted for Indian market data.

What's Next
Roadmap

The current focus is finishing coverage on sectors where the NAV framework applies cleanly. Water, cables, recycling, auto metal forming, lending, energy. Reports will be added continuously. Beyond that, additional frameworks for different firm types are in design. Each will get its own scoring system and its own home on this site.

I am also working on backtesting methodologies for the framework. The most immediate test is rebuilding the framework scores using only data available as of 31 December 2024, then observing how those companies actually behaved across calendar 2025. That gives a full year of out-of-sample observation with no hindsight bias. Done honestly, this kind of test will show where the framework actually works as a descriptive lens and where it does not. The results will go on this site, whatever they say. None of it will be a recommendation; it will be data on how a personal framework performed.

Two new AI agents are also in development, extending the pipeline into ongoing watchlist monitoring rather than only initial scoring.

  • A1
    Adverse news monitor. Continuously scans publicly available news and disclosures for companies in my watchlist. Flags governance changes, auditor exits, regulatory actions, and material announcements that should re-trigger a review of how I think about that company.
  • A2
    FII/DII flow monitor. Tracks foreign institutional and domestic institutional flows at the sector and stock level. Material shifts in institutional positioning are useful context for thinking about a company, alongside other inputs.

If anything on this site helps you think more clearly about a company, whether you end up agreeing with my framework or disagreeing with it, that is the entire point. The framework is a thinking tool, not a buy list.
